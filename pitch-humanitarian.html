Cryptographic Proof That Aid Reached a Real Person

IRLid creates tamper-evident delivery receipts using ECDSA signatures — no app, no central server, no biometrics required.

The Problem

How do you prove that supplies, food, or medical equipment reached a specific real person, at a specific location, at a specific time — without trusting a central database, without requiring biometrics, and without infrastructure that can be manipulated or corrupted?

How It Works — Delivery Flow

Step 1
Delivery agent arrives

Field worker or drone displays a signed QR code on their device. The QR contains a cryptographic commitment to their GPS location and timestamp.

Step 2
Recipient scans

Recipient scans with any phone browser — no app needed. Their device signs a cryptographic response binding them to this specific delivery.

Step 3
Receipt created

A tamper-evident receipt is generated. Both parties' signatures are verified. The receipt is independently verifiable by anyone — forever.

What the Receipt Cryptographically Proves

  • Two distinct devices were present and signed
  • Both devices reported the same GPS location (within 12 metres)
  • The exchange happened within a 90-second window
  • Neither signature can be forged without the private key
  • The receipt cannot be backdated or modified after creation
  • Verification requires no trust in any central authority

Privacy by Design

  • No biometric data collected by default — optional face capture stays on-device if enabled
  • No central identity database
  • Private keys never leave the device
  • GPS coordinates are in the receipt by design — both parties attest to them
  • Recipient only needs a phone browser — no account, no registration
  • Receipt is self-verifying — does not depend on IRLid's servers to remain valid

🚁 Autonomous Drone Delivery

For hover-and-drop or landing drone delivery, the drone displays its signed HELLO QR on a screen or printed panel. The recipient scans it with their phone, completing the handshake. The drone's camera can optionally capture the recipient's face alongside the QR as a witness record.

The receipt is stored on-device and synced to the cloud when connectivity is available — making it viable in low-connectivity field environments. No operator needs to be present on-site.

What this proves: A real person with a real device was physically present to receive this specific delivery, at this location, at this time. The drone did not simply drop supplies and fly away unwitnessed.

Honest limitations: GPS coordinates are self-reported by both parties — the protocol does not independently verify location via a trusted GPS source. It provides a tamper-evident record that both parties attested to the delivery. This is the same trust model as a signed witness statement. For adversarial contexts where a recipient might falsify location, additional verification layers (biometric, face capture) are available as optional enhancements. The protocol is open source — audit it at github.com/BunHead/IRLid.
